about me

Statistical Analyst & Software Engineer

I graduated from Washburn University with a BS in mathematics, a concentration in applied statistics, and a minor in computer information science, finishing with a 3.95 GPA. The foundation of that success was straightforward: I found the material genuinely compelling. The intellectual depth of the coursework, and the satisfaction of working through difficult problems, was a consistent source of motivation throughout. Pure mathematics courses such as linear algebra and stochastic processes trained me to recognize structure in abstract problems and approach them from multiple analytical angles, building fluency in eigendecomposition, Markov chain modeling, and the mathematical foundations of Brownian motion from first principles. Applied regression and time series courses translated those tools into practice through AIC/BIC model selection, variance inflation factor analysis, and ARIMA/SARIMA identification, worked through both by hand and in Python and R. Data mining and machine learning coursework in Python spanned both supervised and unsupervised methods, from k-means clustering analyzed through centroid convergence and within-cluster variance, to Bayesian classifiers grounded in prior and posterior distributions, support vector machines, and neural networks. The emphasis throughout was not just on application, but on mechanism: understanding how backpropagation propagates error gradients, how gradient descent minimizes a loss function across iterations, and what it actually means for a model to generalize, which built a concrete picture of what machine learning and modern AI are at a mathematical level. Computer science coursework in algorithm design and database management sharpened my ability to build efficient, well-structured solutions to complex problems, a skill I carried equally into statistical modeling and into my software engineering internship.

Beyond academics, I spent over a year working on a derivatives and investment front office tech team, contributing to production Python codebases that the derivatives team relied on daily. Working alongside experienced engineers shaped my understanding of collaborative software development. Every change I made moved through a formal review process, with merge requests I authored to document not just the code modification, but the implementation SQL, test coverage, and rollback procedure, so both the engineering and data teams could review and deploy with confidence. I developed the discipline of asking well-structured questions by framing what I already understood, what I had already investigated, and where I was stuck before pulling in a senior engineer. Much of my day-to-day work centered on supporting data workflows directly: writing SQL validation queries to confirm that pipeline loads were accurate and complete after code changes, and improving the automated email outputs downstream users depended on, ensuring every alert carried properly structured context and clear warnings. On the engineering side, I reduced a critical data loader's runtime by approximately 87% through vectorization and threading, built containerized internal tooling with Docker, and contributed to cross-platform CI/CD pipeline compatibility between Windows and Linux environments. That experience changed how I approach data work: I think in terms of systems, observability, correctness, and efficiency, not just models.

Outside of code, I am drawn to climate science, the way math shows up in music, how quantitative methods can model psychological and social phenomena, and any dataset that tells a story hiding in apparent noise. I am starting a Master's in Data Science in Fall 2026 and actively looking for full-time roles where statistical depth and engineering discipline meet.

Climate Change Data Analysis

After taking a semester-long environmental science course, I was inspired to build a project that could make the vast, interconnected science of climate change accessible to a general audience. I learned more from that course than almost any other, and I wanted to apply that knowledge to something someone without a scientific background could meaningfully explore. The result is "The Threshold," an interactive web application that maps 19 scientifically documented climate tipping points, the thresholds beyond which a natural system can shift irreversibly. The app lets users explore each tipping point in depth, tracing the cascade of effects that follows when one system tips, from melting ice sheets raising sea levels to collapsing ocean currents disrupting monsoons that feed hundreds of millions of people. A built-in Domino Simulator lets users adjust real-world conditions like emissions pressure, policy response speed, and the spread of misinformation, then watch which tipping points activate and cascade into each other. There are thousands of human activities that contribute to and interact with climate change, but most people are never shown how these forces connect. This project is an attempt to show not just that climate change is happening, but how it cascades, why it accelerates, and what makes it so difficult to stop once it begins.
